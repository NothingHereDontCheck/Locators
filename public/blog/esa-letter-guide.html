ESA Guide

What Is an ESA Letter and Do You Need One?

February 2025· 9 min read· Metro Locators Team

If you have a pet and you rent in Atlanta — or any U.S. city — you're likely paying pet fees that add hundreds of dollars per year to your housing costs. The average Atlanta apartment charges a $350 to $500 pet deposit plus $35 to $75 per month in pet rent. Over a 12-month lease, that's $770 to $1,400 in pet-related costs on top of your base rent.

Many renters are unaware that federal housing law provides a path to eliminate these costs entirely — through an Emotional Support Animal (ESA) letter. Here's everything you need to know about what an ESA is, what legal protections it carries, and how to get a legitimate letter through the right process.

What Is an Emotional Support Animal?

An Emotional Support Animal is a companion animal — any domestic species — that a licensed mental health professional has prescribed to provide therapeutic support to a person with a qualifying emotional or psychological condition. The ESA's value is its presence: the companionship, comfort, and routine it provides to its owner.

ESAs are not the same as service animals, and the distinction matters significantly for renters. Here's how they compare:

Service Animals

  • Trained to perform specific tasks for a person with a disability
  • Protected under the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA)
  • Allowed in virtually all public places: restaurants, stores, planes, hotels
  • Must be a dog (or miniature horse) under ADA
  • No letter required — verbal confirmation allowed

Emotional Support Animals

  • Provide therapeutic benefit through companionship
  • Protected under the Fair Housing Act (FHA)
  • Protected in housing — not automatically in public spaces or planes
  • Any domestic species qualifies
  • Requires a letter from a licensed mental health professional

Your Rights Under the Fair Housing Act

The Fair Housing Act (FHA) prohibits housing discrimination against people with disabilities and requires housing providers to make reasonable accommodations when requested. For ESA owners, this translates into three specific protections that can directly impact your housing costs:

  1. The right to live with your ESA even in a no-pets building. A landlord's "no pets" policy does not apply to certified emotional support animals. If you have a valid ESA letter, the landlord must allow your animal regardless of their general pet policy.
  2. The right to pay no pet deposits. Landlords cannot charge upfront pet deposits for an ESA. The animal is not a "pet" under fair housing law — it's an accommodation for a disability.
  3. The right to pay no monthly pet rent. Monthly pet fees also cannot be charged for an ESA. Your only obligation is the standard rent and any standard security deposit applicable to all tenants.

Important: While landlords cannot deny your ESA or charge pet fees, they can still hold you responsible for any actual damage the animal causes to the property — beyond normal wear and tear. This is standard for all security deposits.

Who May Qualify for an ESA?

You may qualify for an ESA letter if you have a mental or emotional condition that substantially limits one or more major life activities. The key is that a licensed mental health professional (LMHP) — a therapist, psychologist, or psychiatrist — must evaluate your situation and determine that an ESA would provide meaningful therapeutic benefit.

Common qualifying conditions include:

  • Generalized anxiety disorder and social anxiety
  • Depression and major depressive disorder
  • Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD)
  • Panic disorder
  • Bipolar disorder
  • Autism spectrum disorder
  • Attention deficit disorder (ADD/ADHD)
  • Other conditions as evaluated by a licensed professional

You do not need to have an existing diagnosis or a therapist already on file to start the process. The evaluation itself is part of obtaining an ESA letter through services like Pettable.

What Makes an ESA Letter Legitimate?

Unfortunately, the internet is full of ESA "registries," certificates, and vest sellers that offer no real legal protection. Landlords are increasingly aware of these scams, and presenting a fraudulent ESA letter can seriously damage your credibility and even constitute fraud in some states.

A legitimate ESA letter must:

  • Be written on the letterhead of a licensed mental health professional
  • Be signed by an LMHP who is licensed in your state (not just "nationally certified")
  • Include the provider's license number, license type, and contact information
  • State that you are a patient of the provider and have a disability-related need for an ESA
  • Be specific to housing accommodation (a separate letter may be needed for air travel, though ESA airline protections have largely been removed since 2021)

What is not a legitimate ESA letter: online registrations, certificates from ESA "registries," vests or ID cards, or letters from providers who are not licensed in your state. These have no legal standing under the Fair Housing Act.

How Much Can an ESA Letter Save You?

Let's look at a realistic Atlanta example. You're renting a $1,400/month one-bedroom and you have a dog:

  • Pet deposit: $400 (one-time, at move-in)
  • Monthly pet rent: $50/month × 12 months = $600/year
  • Total first-year pet cost: $1,000

With a valid ESA letter, both of these charges are eliminated. The letter itself typically costs between $100 and $200 through a reputable service — meaning you could break even within the first two months and save $800 or more in year one alone.

Presenting Your ESA Letter to a Landlord

Once you have your ESA letter, the process for presenting it to a landlord is straightforward. You are not required to disclose your specific diagnosis — only that you have a disability and that a licensed professional has recommended an ESA. The landlord may verify the letter with the provider listed on it, but cannot require you to provide medical records.

Submit your ESA letter before or during the application process to avoid being charged pet fees that you'd then need to dispute. Most landlords and property managers are familiar with ESA accommodations and will process your request without issue once they've verified the letter's legitimacy.
